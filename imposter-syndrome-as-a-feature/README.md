# Imposter syndrome as a feature

The signal inside the doubt.

<img src="./static/article-01.png" width="100%"></img>

> You’re handed a system and suddenly the decision is yours.
> Now, you're less certain than you thought.
> Not because you're incapable, but because you understand the weight of being wrong.

That feeling has a name, if you haven’t felt it yet... you probably will.
It tends to show up when you're stretching.

A bigger scope.
More responsibility.

## The edge of understanding

<img src="./static/article-02.png" align="right" width="21.5%"></img>

That discomfort isn’t incompetence. It’s awareness. It’s the moment you realize you’re operating close to the edge of what you actually understand. For most of our careers, that edge moved slowly. You learned a new framework, a pattern, then another. You took on a little more responsibility. Confidence expanded gradually, more or less in step with experience.

Now, it feels that the ground itself is shifting.

Programming languages allowed us to build abstractions. Over time, you learn to take something messy and shape it into something stable. You extract patterns, encode them into structures, and leave behind something that reflects how you think about the system.

That process creates a sense of authorship.

Prompting feels different, our relationship changed.

> If a machine can generate the "visible" part of the work so fast, what part still belongs to you?

That question isn’t really about productivity.

Coding was never just typing. It was also how you built confidence. Writing something that didn’t work. Refactoring it. Understanding why it broke.

---

### curiosity && write().test().understand().refactor();

For most engineers, confidence did not come from shipping code quickly. It came from repeatedly moving through the same cycle: writing something, testing it, understanding why it behaved the way it did, and refining it. That loop builds intuition. But people from outside had a different perspective, "coding" took too long. But when the “understand” step is skipped, something subtle changes. You can still ship "features", You describe something: It appears. And who knows? even the tests might pass. But the underlying mental model died.

That's where imposter syndrome becomes sharper.

Being asked to explain a decision you did not fully unpack is one of the fastest ways to feel exposed.
This is one reason why “vibe coding” feels exciting but also strangely hollow. It removes friction. But that's where most understanding is built.

The craft didn’t disappear.

- If your confidence came from speed, yes — it's not the same anymore.
- Being the person who remembered the syntax used to matter more. It was satisfying (and most of/some-times good for the ego)

That was only the surface.

The real work was always choosing the right problem, understanding trade-offs, seeing second-order effects, recognizing when something feels off and more importantly: keeping humans aligned around ambiguity. AI predicts the most likely sequence of words and that’s powerful, it's a great tool. But it’s not the same thing as understanding the system it is modifying.

A model can suggest an implementation, but it doesn’t carry the consequences of that implementation once it becomes part of a real system. It doesn’t live with the trade-offs, it doesn’t understand.

## Responsibility is deterministic

<img src="./static/article-03.png" align="left" width="22%"></img>

Engineers are trusted with problems before they feel fully ready for them. They step into systems they did not design. They make decisions without having perfect information. Over time, certainty grows around that responsibility.
AI has not changed it. If anything, it has made the edge of the craft easier to see.

When generating code becomes trivial, the parts of the job that actually matter become clearer: understanding context, making trade-offs, deciding what should exist in the first place.

You have to take out pieces that doesn't make sense anymore. Keep it stable.

> The feeling many engineers try to eliminate may actually be serving a useful purpose.

It keeps you careful.

It keeps you curious.

It keeps you honest about what you do and do not yet understand.

It's not a bug, it's feature.
